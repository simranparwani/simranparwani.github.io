Week 7: Capstone Reflection

I’ve usually been able to separate my academic life and my personal life. However, Capstone seems to take all these aspects of life and smush them together like a glob of play-doh, devastating any semblance I had of order and control. I think that’s why it has been so difficult for me to reflect on how Capstone is going because it feels so intertwined with everything else in my life and so all I can muster to say is that I’m hanging in there.

Rereading this first paragraph makes me want to laugh in a way. Why has Capstone been elevated to such an angsty dramatic level when it is, in reality, just like any other IM project at its core? Why do I synonymize Capstone and creativity and place creativity at a dangerously elusive pedestal when it is a daily practice? Why do I spend so much time thinking about Capstone as a concept instead of actually working on it?

I felt much better about the process after last week--after doing my extended critique with the class and having another meeting with my capstone mentor, Jonny. After writing the midterm paper about my inspirations, I found it easier to pinpoint what exactly I wanted to gather from the projects that inspire me. From Giorgia Lupi’s Data Humanism work, I wanted to explore how one could evoke empathy through a data visualization. From Gwendolyn Warren’s counter data project with Black youth in Detroit and her powerful visualization of commuters running over Black children in downtown Detroit, I hope to explore data activism--where this empathy through visualization can inspire action. From Catherine D’Ignazio’s art and research, I hope to explore how marginalized communities can reclaim control of data and subvert what institutions decide is important. From Shirley Wu’s ITP installation and accompanying web piece, I hope to explore what a visualization could look like in both physical computing and web. Taking these goals together, I feel like I have a strong idea of the underlying values for my project and the effect I want it to have.

I presented this to the class last week during my extended critique and was happy to hear that my ideas were communicated clearly. One important piece of feedback I received was to make it clear what the exact action I want my piece to inspire in terms of the “data activism” part of my project. I also just appreciated the wholesomeness of the class talking me through my anxieties.

Here is a summary of what I’ve worked on so far:

  • Prototyped a playful data visualization exploring consolidation of local newspapers in Ohio
  • Re-iterated that prototype with leading the user through the story of it using scrollytelling techniques
  • Did a counterdata project to collect data about NYUAD Convenience Store prices. Instead of doing a visualization, I coded a game to help students actually experience how the data affects them → turned it into a Gazelle article
  • Read Data Feminism by Lauren Klein and Catherine D’Ignazio and Dear Data by Giorgia Lupi
  • Building an LED lightshow to visualize the influence of female Nobel Prize winners

I felt “stuck” when I met with Jonny last week. I had the structure and values of what I wanted to do, but I didn’t have the substance that created that structure. Talking to him gave me some good ideas about moving forward. He also described a couple of projects he has worked on and talked me through his process of “narrowing focus,” which I think is the hardest part of the creative process. He also suggested reading a couple of books that were slightly unrelated to my topic to help inspire me out of this rut, such as a French philosophy book and a book called Glitch Feminism given my interest in the Glitch Studies Manifesto I had read as a sophomore in his Circuit Breakers! class.

Through building the visualization of Ohio newspaper ownership, I’ve learned that I want to build visualizations where the user can “play” with the data. I also learned what are some ways you can make visualizations less dependent on the external legend and make it more intuitive.

Through doing the mini project on the NYUAD convenience store prices, I’ve learned about building my own community datasets and designing interactions that allow the user to experience the data in their own context.

Through reading Dear Data, my understanding of data and the traditional methods associated with it has been completely transformed. I now question: What data do we find meaningful? Why must we aggregate data into visualizations that can be interpreted quickly? How can we use data as a way to understand ourselves and the people around us?

Through reading Data Feminism, I’ve learned so much about the logic behind the gendered binary of emotion and reason and the existing thoughts in the data viz community about the importance of visual minimalism and objectivity in visualization. I also learned about how important it is to consider intersectionality in data.

Through building the LED lightshow, I have learned how demotivating it is to do physical computing right now and the difficulty of experimenting.

This week I hope to explore what might empathetic visualization look like in a journalism setting. The data was obtained from the University of North Carolina's Center for Innovation and Sustainability in Local Media's Database of Newspapers. This dataset shows the newspapers open in Ohio in the years 2004, 2014, 2016, and 2019. I had cleaned the data in a previous CS class as a practice assignment. I wanted to create a visualization to show the consolidation of local newspapers in Ohio. Thus, my goal was to show the change in the number of newspapers per year and show the change in how many newspapers each owner had. I wanted something that showed connections between papers and owners like a network graph.

While I like the floral undertones of the network graph and the way nodes can "bounce back", it's not very easy to see the changes in network graphs over time. I also wanted something that was a bit more playful. I decided to create something that was a mix between a force directed network graph and a clustered bubble chart. Here's the prototype that I came up with.


I like how the user can move the circles and also get more information if they hover over them. If the user uses the slider, they can see the ownership of Ohio newspapers each available year. I think its pretty fun to interact with.

I user tested this with a friend and realized how dependent the visualization was on the legend. How might I redesign it for people who use screen readers and navigate with keyboards? Additionally, I think there will be the tendency to skip reading the legend and just move to the visualization...in what ways can I integrate better affordances into the design of the visualization?
